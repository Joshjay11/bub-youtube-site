# MODULE 0: IDEA VALIDATOR

Before you write a single word of script, validate the idea. Mid-tier creators waste 30-40% of their scripting time on ideas that were never going to work. This module kills bad ideas fast.

---

## 0A. Idea Scorecard

Score every video concept before committing to it. If it doesn't hit GO, don't waste 4 hours finding out the hard way.

Rate each criterion 1-5:

| Criterion | What You're Scoring | 1 (Weak) | 5 (Strong) |
|-----------|-------------------|----------|------------|
| Curiosity | Would a stranger click this? | Generic topic, no surprise | Specific claim that demands an answer |
| Audience Relevance | Does YOUR audience need this? | Tangentially related | They're asking for this in comments |
| Novelty | Has this angle been done to death? | 10 videos with this exact take exist | Nobody has approached it this way |
| Proof Available | Can you back it up? | Opinion only, no data | Studies, examples, receipts |
| Emotional Tension | Does it create a feeling? | Pure information transfer | Makes them angry, hopeful, or curious |
| Title Potential | Can you write a title with a knowledge gap? | Descriptive but flat | You'd click it yourself |
| Thumbnail Potential | Can you make a thumb that stops scrolling? | Text-heavy, no visual hook | Clear visual concept, high contrast |
| Satisfaction Potential | Will viewers feel the video delivered? | Vague payoff, lots of filler | Crystal clear delivery on promise |
| Production Feasibility | Can you actually make this? | Requires assets you don't have | Fully within your capability |

**Scoring:**
- 40+: **GO** — Script it.
- 30-39: **HOLD** — Revisit monthly. Sometimes the timing is wrong, not the idea.
- Below 30: **KILL** — Archive it. Don't delete — your worst idea today might be perfect in 6 months when the context changes.

---

## 0B. Viewer Belief Map

Don't build a generic audience avatar. Map what's happening inside your viewer's HEAD for THIS specific video.

**Before they click:**
- What do they currently believe about this topic?
- What are they skeptical about?
- What do they fear won't work or isn't true?
- What do they secretly hope IS true?
- What evidence would change their mind?

**After they watch:**
- What should they now believe?
- What specific emotion should they feel? (not "informed" — be precise: relieved? angry? motivated? unsettled?)
- What should they do next?

**The Gap:**
The distance between "before" and "after" is the value of your video. If the gap is small, the video isn't worth making. If the gap is huge but you can't bridge it in one video, you need to narrow your scope.

## 1B. Video-Specific Audience Snapshot

This is NOT your channel audience avatar. This is for THIS video specifically.

1. **What did this viewer just watch or Google before finding your video?** (This tells you their mental state and expectations.)

2. **What's their expertise level ON THIS TOPIC?**
   - Unaware: They don't know this problem or topic exists
   - Aware: They've seen other videos but don't have a framework
   - Informed: They know the basics and hit a wall
   - Advanced: They need the deep cut nobody else provides

3. **What's the one thing they'd be disappointed NOT to learn?** (This is your non-negotiable payoff. Everything else is structure.)

4. **What would make them comment?** (Not just watch — comment. This tells you the emotional charge of the topic.)

5. **Are they a returning subscriber or a new viewer?** (This changes the hook. Subscribers tolerate 5 seconds of context. New viewers give you 2 seconds.)

## 1F. Framing Worksheet

The same topic can be framed 10 different ways. This forces you to pick the strongest one.

**Frame it 5 ways:**

1. As a story: "This is the story of..."
2. As a mystery: "Nobody can explain why..."
3. As a contradiction: "Everything you've been told about [X] is wrong because..."
4. As a how-to: "Here's exactly how to..."
5. As a debate: "One side says [X]. The other says [Y]. The truth is..."

**Decision Criteria (pick the frame that wins the most):**
- Which frame does my title/thumbnail support best?
- Which frame does my research support with the strongest evidence?
- Which frame would my audience NOT expect from me? (novelty wins)
- Which frame can I execute in my production budget and timeline?

If two frames tie: pick the one the audience wouldn't expect.

## 2D. The 35% Pivot — Explained

The most important structural beat in any YouTube video. If your retention dips in the middle, this is almost always what's missing.

**What it is:** The moment the video shifts from setup to reframe. The viewer thought the video was about X. At the 35% mark, it becomes about X through the lens of Y. This resets the retention clock because the viewer's curiosity is refreshed with a new question they didn't know they had.

**Why 35%:** Attention naturally dips at the one-third mark. If a viewer has been following one thread for 3-4 minutes, they start feeling like they "get it." The pivot says "you don't get it yet" without being condescending. It creates a second wave of curiosity inside a video they were about to passively watch.

**How to find YOUR pivot:** Take your topic. Ask: what's the adjacent field that explains WHY this topic works the way it does? The pivot is almost always "the mechanism behind the surface."

**8 Examples:**

**Finance:** The video was about compound interest. At 35%, it became about compound interest through the lens of behavioral psychology — why KNOWING about compound interest doesn't change anyone's behavior. The pivot: "The math isn't the problem. Your brain is."

**Fitness:** The video was about protein intake. At 35%, it became about who funds the studies that set the protein recommendations — and why the supplement industry has a $50B reason to keep the numbers high. The pivot: "The science is real. The dosage isn't."

**Tech:** The video was about iPhone vs Android. At 35%, it became about how your phone choice reveals your relationship with control and privacy — and what that says about how tech companies see you. The pivot: "This isn't about specs. It's about what you're willing to give up."

**History:** The video was about the fall of Rome. At 35%, it became about the specific economic pattern that Rome, the British Empire, and one modern superpower all share — and whether the pattern is breakable. The pivot: "This isn't ancient history. It's a forecast."

**Cooking:** The video was about making perfect pasta. At 35%, it became about the single chemistry principle that explains why restaurant pasta tastes different from yours — and why the internet gets it wrong. The pivot: "It's not the recipe. It's the water."

**Commentary:** The video was about why movies are getting worse. At 35%, it became about the financial structure that makes studios optimize for opening weekend, not quality — and why streaming made it worse, not better. The pivot: "The movies aren't the product. The opening weekend number is."

**Tutorial:** The video was about Excel formulas. At 35%, it became about the 3 formulas that replace 90% of what people use VLOOKUP for — and why every Excel tutorial teaches the slow way first. The pivot: "Forget everything you just learned. Here's how people who use Excel 8 hours a day actually do it."

**Documentary:** The video was about a missing persons case. At 35%, it became about the specific police policy that guarantees cases like this go cold — and the 4 other cases where the same policy produced the same result. The pivot: "The detectives didn't fail. The system did."

## 2E. Hook Formula Bank

15 fill-in-the-blank formulas grouped by type.

### Contradiction Hooks (Highest CTR)
1. "Everyone says [common belief]. The data says the exact opposite."
2. "[Authority figure/institution] spent [specific amount] proving that [surprising claim]."
3. "The [number] most [adjective] [people/companies] in the world all do [unexpected thing]. Here's why."

### Question Hooks
4. "What if [accepted truth] was actually [opposite]?"
5. "Why does [specific surprising thing] happen, and what does it mean for [viewer's concern]?"
6. "How is it possible that [contradictory fact]?"

### Story Hooks (Cold Opens)
7. "It's [year]. [Place]. [Person] is about to [do something that changes everything]."
8. "[Specific vivid detail]. That was the moment [person] realized [everything changed]."
9. "This [object/document/place] sat [hidden/forgotten/classified] for [time period]. When they finally [found/released/opened] it..."

### Data Hooks
10. "[Specific number/percentage] of [group] [surprising behavior]. And nobody is talking about why."
11. "A study of [large number] [things] found something that contradicts everything we assumed about [topic]."

### Challenge Hooks
12. "I tried [thing] for [time period]. Here's what actually happened."
13. "They told me [common advice]. I did the opposite. [Result]."

### Stakes Hooks
14. "In [time period], [something dramatic] will change about [thing viewer cares about]. Most people aren't ready."
15. "There's a [number]% chance you're doing [common activity] wrong. Here's the test."

## 2F. Bad / Better / Best Hook Examples

For each hook type, here's what a 3/10, 6/10, and 9/10 looks like — and why.

### Contradiction

**Bad (3/10):** "Today I'll explain why everything you know about productivity is wrong."
Why it fails: "Everything you know" is too broad. "Today I'll explain" is throat-clearing. No specificity.

**Better (6/10):** "The most productive people in the world don't use to-do lists. Here's what they do instead."
Why it's better: Specific claim (no to-do lists). Creates curiosity (what DO they use?). But "most productive" is vague — who? Prove it.

**Best (9/10):** "I tracked 200 CEOs for a year. The ones who got the most done had one thing in common — and it's not discipline, focus, or morning routines."
Why it works: Specific data (200 CEOs, one year). Rules out the obvious answers. The viewer HAS to know. Credibility baked in.

### Question

**Bad (3/10):** "Have you ever wondered why some thumbnails get more clicks?"
Why it fails: Low specificity. "Some thumbnails" is vague. No stakes.

**Better (6/10):** "Why do some thumbnails get 10% CTR while others get 2%? I tested 50 to find out."
Why it's better: Numbers. Personal experiment. But "I tested 50" is modest — not enough to feel authoritative.

**Best (9/10):** "Why do some thumbnails get 10% CTR? I tested 200 across 6 niches and found a pattern that contradicts what every thumbnail expert says."
Why it works: Larger dataset. Cross-niche (generalizable). "Contradicts experts" creates tension. The viewer now has TWO curiosity gaps — the pattern AND why experts are wrong.

### Cold Open / Story

**Bad (3/10):** "So I was at this conference last week and something interesting happened."
Why it fails: No visual. No stakes. "Something interesting" is the viewer's job to decide, not yours.

**Better (6/10):** "Three months ago, a researcher walked into a lab in Zurich and ran an experiment that should have been impossible."
Why it's better: Specificity (Zurich, three months ago). "Should have been impossible" creates stakes. But we don't know WHY we should care yet.

**Best (9/10):** "The room was empty except for a single monitor showing a number nobody expected. The researcher checked it twice. Then she called her supervisor. Then the supervisor called the press. Within 48 hours, three governments were scrambling."
Why it works: Cinematic. Escalating stakes in real-time. You're IN the scene. Multiple layers of reaction confirm this is important before you even know what happened.

### Data

**Bad (3/10):** "Statistics show that most people fail at their goals."
Why it fails: "Statistics show" is the weakest attribution in language. "Most people" and "goals" are both vague.

**Better (6/10):** "92% of New Year's resolutions fail by February. But there's a psychological trick that flips those odds."
Why it's better: Specific number. Specific timeframe. Promise of a solution.

**Best (9/10):** "A 40-year longitudinal study at Harvard tracked 724 people from their twenties to their eighties. The single strongest predictor of whether they were healthy and happy had nothing to do with money, career, or exercise."
Why it works: Massive authority (Harvard, 40 years, 724 people). Rules out three obvious answers. The viewer literally cannot not click.

### Challenge

**Bad (3/10):** "I decided to try something different this month."
Why it fails: No specificity. No stakes. "Something different" means nothing.

**Better (6/10):** "I deleted all social media for 30 days. Here's what happened to my productivity."
Why it's better: Clear experiment. Time-bound. Measurable outcome.

**Best (9/10):** "I gave $50,000 of my company's ad budget to an AI and told it to run everything for 90 days. I wasn't allowed to touch a single campaign. The results broke three things I believed about marketing."
Why it works: Real money (stakes). Constraint (wasn't allowed to touch). Specific outcome (broke three beliefs). The risk is tangible.

### Stakes

**Bad (3/10):** "This is something you should really pay attention to."
Why it fails: Telling someone to care doesn't make them care. No specificity.

**Better (6/10):** "In 2026, YouTube is changing how it recommends videos. If you're a creator, this affects you starting next month."
Why it's better: Specific platform, specific year, specific timeline. But "this affects you" is still vague — HOW?

**Best (9/10):** "In 47 days, YouTube will finish rolling out a system that makes watch time less important than viewer satisfaction. Channels optimized for watch time are about to see their traffic cut in half. Here's how to be on the right side of it."
Why it works: Exact number (47 days). Specific consequence (traffic cut in half). Clear division (right side vs. wrong side). Urgency without clickbait.

## 2G. Hook Strength Scorecard

After writing your hook, score it. 1 point per criterion.

1. [ ] Opens mid-action or mid-thought (not "Hey guys" or "In this video")
2. [ ] Creates a SPECIFIC curiosity gap (not a vague one)
3. [ ] Contains a number, name, or concrete detail
4. [ ] Makes a promise the video actually keeps
5. [ ] Excludes people who wouldn't like this video (you want qualified viewers)
6. [ ] Avoids starting with "I" or "So"
7. [ ] Creates stakes (why should I care RIGHT NOW?)
8. [ ] Is under 90 words
9. [ ] Matches the title/thumbnail promise
10. [ ] Would make YOU stop scrolling if someone else said it

**Score:**
- 8-10: Ship it.
- 5-7: Revise. One or two elements are weak.
- Below 5: Start over. The concept isn't working, not just the wording.

## 2H. Satisfaction Engineering Checklist

The 2026 algorithm rewards how viewers FEEL after watching, not just how long they watched. Run this before publishing.

- [ ] Title promise is visually confirmed in the first 10 seconds
- [ ] The "proof of payoff" moment happens at the 35% mark, not the end
- [ ] A viewer who leaves at 50% still got value (good abandonment)
- [ ] The video answers the core question completely (no cliffhanger bait)
- [ ] The viewer knows what to DO with this information
- [ ] A new viewer would trust this channel after watching
- [ ] The comment prompt encourages specific responses ("which of these three applies to you?" not "what do you think?")
- [ ] The ending feels complete, not abrupt

# MODULE 3: AI PROMPT TEMPLATES

## How to Use These Prompts

These prompts are designed for Claude, GPT, Gemini, or any AI assistant. They are thinking-partner prompts that help you brainstorm, outline, and refine. They are NOT "make me a script" prompts.

**Rules:**
1. Always edit the AI output. Never use it raw.
2. The AI is your research assistant and brainstorming partner, not your writer.
3. If the output sounds like "a YouTube script," throw it away and try again.
4. Your voice, your opinions, your personality go in AFTER the AI helps with structure.

---

## 3A. Find the Angle

**Prompt:**
```
I'm making a YouTube video about [topic]. Here's what every other
creator says about it: [obvious take]. I need a fresh angle that
my audience hasn't heard before.

My audience is: [description from Viewer Belief Map]
My channel's perspective is: [your unique lens]

Give me 5 angles that are NOT the obvious take. For each one,
tell me: what's the hook, what's the surprising insight, and
why would someone who's already seen 3 videos on this topic
click on THIS one?
```

**What good output looks like:** 5 distinct angles where at least 2 make you think "I haven't seen that before." The hooks should be specific, not generic. If any angle starts with "Explore the fascinating world of..." throw it away.

**Red flags:** All 5 angles are variations of the same idea. The hooks are vague ("discover the truth about..."). The angles require expertise you don't have.

**If the output is weak:** Add more context about what your audience already believes (from Viewer Belief Map) and what competitors have already covered (from Module 1C).

---

## 3B. Find the Cross-Disciplinary Connection

**Prompt:**
```
My video is about [Topic A]. I want to find an unexpected
connection to a completely different field that makes the
viewer say "I never thought about it that way."

What does [Topic A] have in common with:
- A concept from psychology?
- A historical event most people don't know?
- A principle from a totally unrelated industry?
- A scientific finding that contradicts common sense?

Give me the 3 strongest connections with a 2-sentence
explanation of why each one works.
```

**What good output looks like:** At least one connection that surprises YOU. The connections should be specific (not "psychology says people are irrational" but "the IKEA effect explains why viewers who participate in your content retain 40% more").

**Red flags:** Connections are surface-level analogies. "It's like..." comparisons that don't add insight.

---

## 3C. Generate Counter-Arguments

**Prompt:**
```
My video argues that [your thesis]. I need to know the
strongest possible objections to this argument.

Give me the top 5 counter-arguments, ranked by how
convincing they are. For each one:
1. State the objection clearly
2. What evidence supports it?
3. How would I address it in my script without dismissing it?
```

**What good output looks like:** At least one counter-argument that makes you uncomfortable. That's the one you need to address in your script. If none of them bother you, the AI is going easy — re-prompt with "Be more aggressive. What would a smart critic say?"

## 5C. 15 Common Retention Failure Modes

When a video underperforms, diagnose it here. Each failure mode includes what it looks like, why it kills retention, and a before/after fix.

**1. Hook Too Broad**
Looks like: "Today we're going to talk about productivity."
Why it kills: No curiosity gap. No reason to stay.
Fix: "I tracked my output for 90 days using 6 different systems. Only one of them actually worked — and it's the one nobody recommends."

**2. Hook Overpromises**
Looks like: Title says "SHOCKING results" but the content is mildly interesting.
Why it kills: Satisfaction score tanks. Algorithm learns your titles lie.
Fix: Match your emotional intensity. If the content is "interesting," say "interesting." Save "shocking" for when something is actually shocking.

**3. Intro Takes Too Long**
Looks like: 45+ seconds before the first piece of value.
Why it kills: The viewer came for the promise. Every second of context is borrowed time.
Fix: Lead with a micro-payoff in the first 30 seconds. Give them one insight immediately, THEN add context.

**4. Point Is Buried Under Setup**
Looks like: 2 minutes of background before the actual insight.
Why it kills: The viewer feels lectured at, not rewarded.
Fix: State the insight first, then explain why it matters. "The answer is X. Here's how we know, and why it changes everything."

**5. Example Is Too Generic**
Looks like: "Imagine you're someone who wants to be more productive..."
Why it kills: Hypotheticals don't engage. Real stories do.
Fix: "My friend Sarah runs a 7-figure business and can't wake up before 10am. Here's her system."

**6. Transition Feels Random**
Looks like: "Okay, moving on to the next point..."
Why it kills: The viewer feels the structure. Structure should be invisible.
Fix: Use a bridge question: "But that raises a problem. If X is true, then why does Y happen?"

**7. Middle Repeats Itself**
Looks like: The same point made three ways in different sections.
Why it kills: The viewer feels like they're not progressing.
Fix: Each section must teach something NEW. If a section only reinforces a previous point, cut it.

**8. No Pivot at 35%**
Looks like: Single thread from start to finish.
Why it kills: Curiosity expires. One question only sustains attention for 3-4 minutes.
Fix: See Section 2D. Introduce a second question that reframes the first.

**9. Best Content Saved for the End**
Looks like: The most surprising insight is at the 85% mark.
Why it kills: 60% of viewers never see it. Your best work is wasted.
Fix: Put your strongest content at the 50% mark. The ending resolves — it doesn't reveal.

**10. Payoff Arrives Too Late**
Looks like: "So what does all of this mean?" at the 90% mark.
Why it kills: Viewers who stayed that long are already committed. The ones you LOST were the ones who needed the payoff earlier.
Fix: Scatter mini-payoffs throughout. Each section should reward the viewer for staying.

**11. Pacing Is Monotone**
Looks like: Every section is the same length, same energy, same structure.
Why it kills: Predictability is the enemy of attention.
Fix: Alternate dense sections with breathing room. Follow a 90-second deep dive with a 20-second anecdote.

**12. Too Much Hedging**
Looks like: "Maybe," "kind of," "I think," "it's possible that" in every paragraph.
Why it kills: Uncertainty is contagious. If you don't sound sure, the viewer doesn't feel sure.
Fix: State claims directly. Use hedging only when intellectual honesty requires it — and when you do, make the uncertainty interesting ("Nobody knows for sure — and here's why that matters").

**13. Ending Is Abrupt**
Looks like: Final point → "Thanks for watching!" → End screen.
Why it kills: No resolution. Open loops dangling. Viewer feels unsatisfied.
Fix: Resolve every loop. Then bridge: "And that's exactly why [specific next video] matters. I'll link it here."

**14. Script Sounds Written, Not Spoken**
Looks like: Long sentences, passive voice, complex subordinate clauses.
Why it kills: The creator stumbles reading it. The stumble shows on camera.
Fix: Read it aloud. Every stumble gets rewritten. If you can't say it in one breath, split it.

**15. Fact Without Context**
Looks like: "The market grew by 47% last year."
Why it kills: Numbers without meaning are noise.
Fix: "The market grew by 47% last year — that's faster than crypto in 2017. And nobody saw it coming."

## 6A. Minimum Viable Script (The Fast Path)

For when you need a script done, not perfected. Follow these steps in order. Set a timer for each. When the timer goes off, move to the next step even if you're not "done."

**Step 1: Idea Scorecard (10 minutes)**
Module 0A. Score the idea. If it's below 30, pick a different idea. Don't script something that's DOA.

**Step 2: Viewer Belief Map (10 minutes)**
Module 0B. What do they believe before, what should they believe after.

**Step 3: Research Sprint (30 minutes MAX)**
Set a timer. Find: 3-5 key facts, 1 surprising thing, 1 counter-argument, 1 cross-disciplinary connection. Use the AI prompts in Module 3. When the timer goes off, STOP.

**Decision gate:** If you don't have a clear angle after 30 minutes, either the topic needs more exploration or it's not strong enough. Check your Idea Scorecard again.

**Step 4: Pick Structure + Set Pacing (5 minutes)**
Use the Decision Tree (2A). Pick your template. Run the Pacing Calculator (4A). You now have word count targets.

**Step 5: Write the Hook (15 minutes)**
Use the Hook Formula Bank (2E). Write 2-3 versions. Score them on the Scorecard (2G). Pick the highest scorer.

If you're stuck: skip the hook. Write the body first. The hook reveals itself once you know what the strongest moment is.

**Step 6: Fill the Canvas (30 minutes)**
Use the Script Draft Canvas (4B). Section by section, don't polish. Ugly is fine. Hit the word count targets.

**Step 7: Audit + Read-Aloud (15 minutes)**
Run the MUST PASS items from the Script Ready Audit (5A). Read the whole thing aloud with a stopwatch. Fix every stumble.

**Total: ~2 hours.** Your first few scripts will take longer. By script 5, this becomes muscle memory.
