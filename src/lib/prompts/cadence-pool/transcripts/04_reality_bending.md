# Chapter 2: Technical Tips

This next tip is more on the technical side.

But details are important.

So it’s critical that you use statements in the past tense, as if the thing you want has already happened.

And we’re almost sure you’ve heard something like this before.

And that’s cool, too.

We’re not trying to recreate anything.

The fact is, if you stress “trying,” you will keep on trying.

Even putting your mental vision in the now can be tricky.

Because now, you are not.

And don’t have.

And that sets up immediate conflict.

But when you put it in the immediate past, as if it already happened, then it removes the conflict.

How can there be conflict over what has already happened?

Also, this moves you to the other side of the chessboard.

Because now, all your mental visions are from the place of already having, and doing, and being.

You can even have some fun and daydream in your spare time.

Because you are doing it from the point of already winning.

This is a most advantageous position to be in.

The next tip coming up is also a technical one.

And that is, you will do better to stick to far fewer statements to begin with.

The reason is, it’s easier to turn multiple statements into a micro-story, with you being the star of the show.

You could even draw out a circle.

Chop it up into four slices like a pizza.

Color each slice.

And write a brief one- or two-word phrase on each slice to represent your statement.

Now, throughout your day, visualize your personal color wheel.

Let it slowly spin around in your mind.

And as it slowly spins, see each word imprinted on each slice.

This method works best if you draw and color the circle in yourself.

That way, it is ingrained in your mind’s eye.

But even if you skip that exercise, our point is that it’s easier to string four ideas together.

It’s easier to remember them.

Like a haiku.

And you will find that they just flow better in your mind.
